
1. Evaluate the subject clarity of the cat in the photograph. Determine whether the cat is in focus, visually distinct from its surroundings, and immediately recognizable as the center of the image. The cat should command the viewer's attention at first glance without requiring searching, squinting, or zooming in. Consider whether the cat is large enough in the frame to register as the subject, whether it contrasts sufficiently against its surroundings rather than blending in, and whether any competing visual elements pull the viewer's eye away from the cat. A photograph where the cat is the first thing a viewer notices and the last thing they forget demonstrates strong subject clarity.

2. Evaluate the intentional framing of the photograph. Determine whether the composition feels deliberate and whether the cat is positioned within the frame in a way that draws the viewer's eye naturally toward it. Consider whether the cat is awkwardly cropped at the edges or lost in excessive empty space, whether leading lines or natural boundaries guide the eye inward toward the subject, and whether the overall arrangement of elements feels balanced and purposeful. Strong framing means the spatial relationship between the cat and its surrounding space serves the cat as the focal point — the composition works with the subject rather than against it, making the photograph feel like a portrait of a cat rather than an image that merely happens to contain one.

3. Evaluate the light and environment of the photograph. Determine whether the lighting reveals the cat's features, texture, and form without harsh overexposure or impenetrable shadow, and whether the background and surrounding space support the subject rather than competing with it. Consider whether the lighting serves the cat naturally, whether the background is clean and non-distracting or cluttered and chaotic, and whether the overall visual setting creates a sense of harmony around the subject. A simple or imperfect setting can score well if the light is effective and the immediate background behind the cat is visually calm, while even a beautiful setting can fail if busy patterns, harsh light, or visual noise fracture the viewer's attention away from the cat.
